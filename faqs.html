Frequently Asked Questions

Common questions about courses, grading, and expectations

Am I allowed to use ChatGPT and other LLM/AI tools?

Yes! AI tools like ChatGPT are allowed and even encouraged in my courses. However, it's important to understand how to use them effectively:

  • Learning tool, not replacement: AI tools should help you understand concepts, not replace your thinking. Use them to explain difficult topics, generate examples, or debug code.
  • Demonstrate understanding: In your submissions, you need to show that you understand what you're submitting. Simply copying AI output without understanding it won't help you learn.
  • Cite when appropriate: If AI significantly contributed to your work, mention it in your reflection or documentation.
  • Remember the goal: Learning happens in your head. AI is a tool to facilitate that learning, like a textbook or tutorial.
How will I be graded?

Grading in my courses focuses on demonstrating mastery and progress rather than one-time performance:

  • Project-based and competency-based: Most assessments are either online resources or project-based, allowing you to demonstrate specific competencies.
  • Multiple opportunities: You won't receive a terminal grade after one attempt. You'll have opportunities to improve based on feedback.
  • Evidence of learning: Your grade reflects the effort and engagement you demonstrate through participation, submissions, and reflections.
  • Individual progress: Success takes into account where you started and how far you've progressed.

Check your specific course page for the exact breakdown (typically 50% skills demonstrations and 50% collection of work).

How can I improve my grade?

There are several ways to improve your grade throughout the course:

  • Engage with feedback: Read and apply the feedback you receive on assignments and projects.
  • Revise and resubmit: Most assignments can be revised and improved. Show that you've learned from feedback.
  • Submit reflections: Reflections help me understand your learning process and where you might need support.
  • Communicate: If you're struggling, reach out. The more I understand your situation, the better I can help.
  • Demonstrate effort: Consistent engagement with course materials and regular submissions show your commitment to learning.
  • Complete optional work: Going beyond minimum requirements demonstrates deeper understanding.
Do I have to do reflections?

Reflections are an important part of the learning process:

  • Required component: Yes, reflections are part of your assessment and help demonstrate your learning journey.
  • Purpose: They help you think about what you've learned, what was challenging, and how you've grown.
  • Communication tool: Reflections help me understand where you might need extra support or resources.
  • Format: Usually brief - focus on quality over quantity. What did you learn? What was difficult? What changed?
  • Frequency: Typically at the end of each class (intention setting at the start, reflection at the end).
Why are there no due dates on assignments?

The flexible approach to deadlines is intentional:

  • Self-paced learning: Everyone learns at different speeds. You can work at a pace that suits your understanding.
  • Real-world skills: Managing your own time and priorities is a valuable professional skill.
  • Quality over speed: Focus on truly understanding the material rather than rushing to meet arbitrary deadlines.
  • Communication is key: While there aren't strict due dates, you should communicate your progress regularly through check-ins and project updates.
  • Course completion: Keep in mind there are still overall course timelines and semester end dates to work within.
What does a Check-In entail?

Check-ins are informal progress conversations:

  • Purpose: To discuss your progress, challenges, and next steps.
  • Format: Usually a brief 1-on-1 conversation during class time or office hours.
  • What to prepare: Be ready to discuss what you've been working on, what's going well, and where you're stuck.
  • Feedback opportunity: A chance to get personalized guidance and clarify any confusion.
  • Not an evaluation: It's a supportive conversation, not a formal assessment.
How much do I have to write for a project update?

Project updates should be concise but informative:

  • Quality over quantity: A few well-thought-out sentences are better than pages of filler.
  • Key information: What have you done? What are you working on next? Any blockers or challenges?
  • Typical length: Usually a paragraph or two (3-5 sentences) is sufficient.
  • Be specific: Instead of "working on the assignment," say "implemented the user login function and started on database integration."
  • Regular updates: Brief regular updates are more valuable than occasional long ones.
How do I know when a project is done?

A project is complete when it meets the learning objectives:

  • Check the brief: Does your work address all the requirements in the assignment brief?
  • Demonstrate competencies: Have you shown mastery of the specific skills the project is meant to assess?
  • Minimum viable product first: Get the core requirements working before adding extras.
  • Reflection component: Most projects include a reflection on what you learned.
  • When in doubt, ask: Submit what you have and ask for feedback if you're unsure.
  • Iterative improvement: Remember, you can improve and resubmit based on feedback.
Will there be any quizzes or tests?

The assessment approach varies by course:

  • Project-focused: Most courses emphasize project-based assessments over traditional tests.
  • Skills demonstrations: Some courses include in-class skills demonstrations (typically 50% of grade).
  • Online assessments: Some courses may use online resources for competency checks.
  • No surprise tests: You'll always know assessment format well in advance.
  • Course-specific: Check your individual course page for the specific assessment breakdown.
Do I have to use the online resources you recommend?

Recommended resources are suggestions, not requirements:

  • Multiple paths to learning: There are many ways to learn the material. My recommendations are starting points.
  • Find what works for you: If you find resources that work better for your learning style, use them!
  • Share discoveries: If you find great resources, share them with the class.
  • Goal is understanding: What matters is that you learn the material, not how you learned it.
  • Ask for alternatives: If recommended resources don't work for you, let me know and I can suggest alternatives.
