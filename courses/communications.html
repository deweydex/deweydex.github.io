Introductory Task (1h)

On a new word doc, let's think through a few things involving communication with clients based on the following scenario:

A new local restaurant who you have never worked with before would like to commission your work (paintings, tableware, statue, mosaics, other endeavour relevant to your practice).

  1. What is the scope of the project? What will you be creating for them? If that has not yet been decided, how will you go about determining that?
  2. How will you be billing this person? How will you ascertain pricing and budgeting?
  3. What information do you need to get started on the project? Assume you do not have the details.
  4. Propose a meeting (time, day etc.) and prepare an agenda for the meeting.

Looking at an Example Together

Let's use the following prompts to discuss a YouTube Video together: as we watch through, see if you can address these points individually:

  • What do you notice about the tone of the speaker?
  • What do you notice about the visuals, the focus, the lighting (cinematography)?
  • What do you notice about how the speaker refers to themselves, to the audience?
  • What emotions do you think the creator is trying to evoke in their viewers? What emotions do you actually feel?
  • What do you notice about the structure of the video, of each scene?
  • What vocabulary do you see the creator use, what level (or register) are they speaking in?
  • Who do you think the primary audience is for this video?

October 6 & 13: Grants & Residencies Research

Overview

Today we will be investigating some funding, learning and travel opportunities associated with your practice. If you are not sure if you want to do these, that's okay, learning about what is out there and figuring out what you are not interested in can be just as important as finding your thing!

Several artists I know have talked about residencies or grants as being what made them decide to go full time, or to change their practice to being alongside a more traditional 9-5. Everyone is different—the key for us here is finding out what is out there and maybe changing our idea about what is possible or realistic.

Grants & Residencies Research Activity

This activity asks you to research real funding and residency opportunities that exist for artists in Ireland and internationally. The goal is to demystify the grants landscape and help you understand what kinds of support are actually available.

Why This Matters

Grants and residencies can provide:

  • Time: Dedicated space to develop work without the pressure of immediate sales
  • Money: Materials budgets, living stipends, or both
  • Space: Access to specialized equipment, studios, or locations
  • Community: Connection with other artists, mentors, or new audiences
  • Legitimacy: Recognition that strengthens future applications and your CV
  • Learning: Opportunities to develop new skills or techniques

The Task

Research grants and residency opportunities from the following categories and select THREE different opportunities that interest you. At least one should be based in Ireland.

Research Sources:
  • Irish National Level: Arts Council of Ireland / An Chomhairle Ealaíon
  • Local Council & Libraries: Dublin City Council Arts Office, your local county council
  • Dublin Studios & Organizations: Fire Station Artists' Studios, Temple Bar Gallery + Studios, Block T
  • Irish Residencies: Tyrone Guthrie Centre, The Mattress Factory, Cill Rialaig Project, Cow House Studios
  • International Residencies: Res Artis, TransArtists, Alliance of Artists Communities, Tokyo Arts and Space, Vermont Studio Center

Research Questions for Each Opportunity

For each of your three selected opportunities, answer thoroughly:

1. Basic Information
  • What is the name of the grant/residency/award?
  • Who is the funding/hosting organization?
  • What is the total value?
  • Application deadlines
  • Website/contact information
2. Eligibility & Experience Level
  • Who is this for?
  • What does "emerging" or "early-career" actually mean to them?
  • Do you need to be a certain age?
  • Geographic restrictions?
  • Previous exhibition history required?
  • Any disciplinary restrictions?
3. What They're Funding
  • For creating new work, or supporting existing projects?
  • Single work or body of work?
  • Must it result in an exhibition? Public presentation?
  • Can it be for research/development without a finished outcome?
4. The Practical Details
  • How long is the grant/residency period?
  • Is it full-time or can you keep other work/commitments?
  • Accommodation provided? Meals? Travel costs?
  • What can the money be spent on?
  • What facilities/equipment are provided?
5. The Strings Attached (Read the Fine Print)
  • Who owns the work you create?
  • Do you retain full copyright?
  • Are you required to donate a work to their collection?
  • What reporting is required?
  • Is there a public outcome required?
6. Application Requirements
  • Written proposal? How long?
  • Portfolio of images? How many?
  • CV/resume?
  • Budget breakdown?
  • Letters of recommendation?
  • What's the selection process?
7. Your Strategic Assessment
  • Why does this opportunity interest you specifically?
  • What would it allow you to do that you can't do now?
  • Do you feel ready to apply now, or is this a future goal?
  • What would you need to develop/prepare to make your application competitive?
  • Are there any red flags or concerns?
  • How does this align with your current practice and goals?

Deliverable Format

Create a document (Word, PDF, or markdown) that includes:

  • Introduction (100-150 words): Brief overview of your current practice and what kinds of support would be most valuable
  • Three Opportunity Profiles (200-500 words each): Address all research questions above
  • Reflection (200-300 words): What surprised you? What did you learn? What patterns did you notice?
