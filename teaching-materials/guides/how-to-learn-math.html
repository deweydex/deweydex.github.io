How to Learn Mathematics

Strategies and approaches for mastering mathematical concepts

What We Learn in School

In high school, mathematics is often presented as a series of algorithms to memorize and procedures to follow. While this approach can help with standardized tests, it rarely builds deep understanding or appreciation for mathematical thinking.

A Different Approach

In my courses, we focus on:

  • Understanding concepts rather than memorizing procedures
  • Seeing patterns and connections between different areas of mathematics
  • Using programming to make abstract concepts concrete
  • Building intuition through visualization and experimentation
  • Asking "why" instead of just "how"

Key Principles

1. Objects and Functions

Think of mathematics as a way of describing objects (numbers, shapes, data) and functions (operations we can perform on those objects). This perspective makes mathematics more like a language for describing patterns.

2. Multiple Representations

Any mathematical concept can usually be represented in multiple ways:

  • Numerically (with numbers and calculations)
  • Graphically (with pictures and diagrams)
  • Algebraically (with symbols and equations)
  • In code (with programs and algorithms)

When you're struggling with a concept in one representation, try looking at it through a different lens. Sometimes seeing a graph makes an equation click, or writing code helps you understand an algorithm.

3. Practice and Exploration

Mathematics is learned through practice, but not mindless repetition. Instead:

  • Start with problems that interest you
  • Experiment and make mistakes
  • Look for patterns in your solutions
  • Create your own practice problems
  • Explain concepts to others

Practical Tips

When You're Stuck

  • Change representations: If algebra isn't working, try drawing a picture
  • Work backwards: Start from the answer and see how you got there
  • Simplify: Try a simpler version of the problem first
  • Ask "why": Why does this formula work? Why do we use this method?
  • Take a break: Sometimes stepping away helps your brain process

Building Intuition

  • Play with examples: Try lots of different numbers, see what happens
  • Look for patterns: What stays the same? What changes?
  • Connect to what you know: How is this like something you've seen before?
  • Visualize: Can you draw this? Graph it? Model it?
  • Use technology: Graphing calculators, Desmos, Python—use tools to explore

The Role of Programming

Programming is an excellent companion to mathematics because:

  • It forces precision—you have to understand exactly what you're doing
  • It provides immediate feedback—you can see if your understanding is correct
  • It makes abstract concepts concrete—you can see patterns emerge
  • It encourages experimentation—changing one number and seeing what happens

Common Misconceptions

"I'm Just Not a Math Person"

This is false. Mathematical ability is not innate—it's developed through practice and the right approach. If you've struggled with math, it's likely because:

  • You were taught procedures without understanding
  • You didn't get enough time to explore and make mistakes
  • The teaching style didn't match your learning style
  • You had a bad experience that made you anxious about math

With the right approach and patience, anyone can learn mathematics.

"I Need to Be Fast at Mental Math"

While mental math can be useful, it's not what mathematics is about. Mathematics is about understanding patterns, solving problems creatively, and reasoning logically. Calculators exist for a reason—use them!

Resources

  • Visual tools: Desmos, GeoGebra
  • Programming: Python, Jupyter notebooks
  • Videos: 3Blue1Brown (highly recommended!), Khan Academy
  • Books: Look for books that explain "why" not just "how"

Remember: Mathematics is a way of thinking, not a collection of formulas to memorize. Focus on understanding, and the techniques will follow.
